What is Covid-19 virus?

- In COVID-19, 'CO' stands for 'corona,' 'VI' for 'virus,' and 'D' for disease. - Formerly, this disease was referred to as “2019 novel coronavirus” or “2019-nCoV”. - There are many types of human coronaviruses including some that commonly cause mild upper-respiratory tract illnesses. - Researchers have used cryogenic electron microscopy to show that coronaviruses enter human cells through an interaction with angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2). ![covid](https://dp9bxf2pat5uz.cloudfront.net/wp-content/uploads/coronavirus-targets-image-750x500.jpg)

Challenges

- A basic challenge confronts all viral therapies: most viruses have just a handful of genes - They rely on proteins in the cells they infect (host cells) to perform many of the functions needed to reproduce. - But therapies that target host cell proteins run the risk of killing uninfected cells, making matters worse. - So antiviral therapies usually target something **unique** about the virus—something important enough that a few mutations in the virus won't make the therapy ineffective.

Making copies of RNA virus

#### Protein Building - Genetic information is carried by DNA - When a protein needs to be built, the relevant bit of DNA is read and the cell makes a temporary copy of the information using a very similar chemical called RNA. - This piece of RNA is then translated into a **sequence of amino acids**, which form the protein. - Exceptions - Many RNAs perform important functions without ever being translated into proteins—all RNA in our cells is made by transcribing a DNA sequence. - Many viruses, including HIV and the influenza virus, use RNA for their basic genetic material. - The coronavirus is also an RNA virus; it consists of a single, 30,000-base-long RNA molecule. - The **host cells** it infects only have proteins that **copy DNA, not RNA**, so how can more copies of the virus get made? - This virus carries its own solution with it to solve this problem - When virus' RNA genome first enters a cell, it interacts with the host's **protein-making machinery** - Uses it to make proteins that can copy RNA molecules.(RNA-copying proteins, called "polymerases) - DNA and RNA are so closely related that it can be difficult to make a drug that affects only one type of polymerase.
